Effort estimation in practice

Planning poker isn't a game — it's a conversation about complexity, assumptions, and risk. Here's how I run it. Click a card to vote.

Select a card to cast your vote. The SM facilitates — not votes first.
How I run estimation
  • Read the story aloud. Ask if anyone needs clarification — before cards go down.
  • Reveal all votes simultaneously. No anchoring.
  • When spread is wide (e.g. 3 vs 13): ask the outliers to speak first. The gap is the conversation.
  • A "?" means the story isn't ready. Stop. Refine it. Don't estimate fiction.
  • Track estimation variance over time — it's a team maturity signal.
Story being estimated
As a finance user, I need to export my consolidated report as a PDF so that I can share it with stakeholders who don't have system access.
Acceptance criteria
  • Export button visible on report summary page
  • PDF includes all visible columns and applied filters
  • Generation completes in under 5 seconds for reports up to 500 rows
  • File named: [ReportName]_[Date]_export.pdf
  • Works in Chrome, Firefox, Edge (latest versions)
Technical considerations surfaced in refinement
PDF library already in stack (confirmed with eng lead). Performance concern at 500+ rows needs spike. Filter state serialization may need separate story. Recommend splitting: core export (5 pts) + performance/large dataset handling (3 pts).

Agile maturity scorecard

The framework I use to assess team maturity at intake — and track progress quarter over quarter. Five dimensions, four levels. Not a judgment; a baseline.

How I use this: Administered as a facilitated team discussion in the first retrospective — not a survey. Each dimension is scored collaboratively, with the SM asking "what does 'optimizing' look like for us?" The output becomes a backlog of process improvement stories, tracked in Jira, with retrospective check-ins every 4 sprints. This is the data behind the team dashboard I send to Ops Management.

SM coaching log

Anonymized entries from real coaching relationships. The work of growing other Scrum Masters — what the sessions look like, what changes.

SM Coachee: "Alex"
Junior SM · 8 months experience · Eptura
Facilitation coaching
Alex's retrospectives were consistently running long and generating lists nobody acted on. Teams were disengaged. The situation: Alex was facilitating retrospectives as brainstorming sessions — no structure, no time-boxing, no owner assignment on actions. I co-facilitated two sessions alongside Alex, then handed the room over and observed. Debriefs focused on one specific technique per session: first, the 5-minute round-robin time-box; then, the "who owns this and by when?" close.
✓ Within 3 sprints, Alex's retros averaged 42 minutes (down from 75+). Action completion rate went from near-zero to ~70%. Alex now mentors a newer SM on the same technique.
SM Coachee: "Jordan"
Mid-level SM · 3 years experience · A&E Networks
Conflict resolution coaching
Jordan was facing a recurring conflict between a Product Owner and a senior engineer — scope disagreements were surfacing in standups, creating team tension and derailing planning sessions. Jordan was avoiding facilitating the conversation directly, defaulting to "taking it offline." I walked Jordan through the three-party facilitation model: name the pattern, not the person; make the process visible; establish an intake agreement before the next planning session. We role-played the conversation twice before Jordan ran it.
✓ Jordan facilitated the three-party session independently. A scope intake agreement was established. Standup dynamics improved within two sprints. Jordan now handles escalation conversations without coaching support.
SM Coachee: "Casey"
New SM transitioning from PM · Paperless Post
Agile mindset coaching
Casey came from a waterfall PM background and was running Scrum with a command-and-control instinct — assigning tasks in standups, tracking individual outputs rather than team velocity, and treating the sprint backlog as a fixed contract rather than a commitment. The change management challenge was significant: Casey's instincts were deeply trained over 5 years of project management. I introduced the concept through artifacts rather than argument — showing the difference between a task board and a story board, then running a side-by-side retrospective on "what did we commit to vs. what did we deliver and why?"
✓ Over 4 months, Casey shifted from task-assignment to impediment-removal facilitation. Sprint goal language replaced ticket-count language in status updates. Casey is now CSM-certified and running two teams independently.

A retrospective on my own practice

Any SM who can't run a retro on themselves isn't really running retros. Here's mine — honest, with owners and due dates on the action items.

✓
What went well
Ceremony quality Standups end on time. Planning ends with a committed, realistic sprint goal. Every ceremony produces an artifact or a decision — not just attendance.
PO partnership & technical collaboration Backlogs stay healthy. I surface technical considerations during refinement by including eng leads in the room. Stories are actionable before they reach planning.
Decisive escalation When a blocker needs VP-level attention, I bring data, a proposed solution, and a recommended timeline — not just the problem. I don't wait to see if things resolve themselves.
Remote relationship-building Every team I've joined remotely has rated psychological safety higher at sprint 6 than sprint 1. I build trust through consistency, follow-through, and being worth showing up for.
△
What I'd improve
Patience with slow adopters I move fast toward good process. Teams operating without discipline for years need more runway than I sometimes give them. Working on it.
Documentation timing I write the thing, then document it. Building the habit of making documentation part of the Definition of Done — not an afterthought.
Delegation vs. doing When something's broken, I fix it. Coaching teams to fix it themselves takes deliberate restraint. Still practicing.

Working agreements — draft v0.1

What I bring to the first team session. A starting point — to be rewritten by the team, together. The SM brings the draft; the team writes the agreement.

01
Ceremonies start and end on time
Respecting time is respecting each other. We don't wait for late arrivals. We don't run over for unprepared ones.
02
Definition of Ready is a gate, not a suggestion
A story without clear acceptance criteria and surfaced technical considerations doesn't enter planning. The eng lead is in refinement. The PO knows this. The team expects it.
03
Blockers surface in standup, not after
If you knew yesterday, say it today. The SM removes blockers — but only the ones we know about. No hero mode.
04
Retro actions have owners and due dates
We don't generate lists and move on. Each action gets a name and a sprint to close. Reviewed at the top of the next retro. No exceptions.
05
Jira is the source of truth
Not Slack, not memory, not email. If it's not in the ticket, it didn't happen. Status updated same day. We don't narrate work that isn't tracked.
06
The SM protects the sprint goal, not individual tickets
New work during a sprint goes through the PO and is evaluated against what we committed to — not absorbed quietly by a willing engineer.
